Reading & Learning 5 May 2025

Why the Books Your Child Reads Shape How They Think

What developmental research says about story-based learning, emotional vocabulary, and why the best children's books do something quite different from what most parents expect.

Ask most parents what they want from their child's books and you'll hear similar answers: imaginative, fun, with a good message at the end. What the research suggests, though, is that the most valuable thing a children's book can do has nothing to do with any of those things.

The books that shape children most deeply are the ones that make them feel seen — and then quietly expand what they think is possible.

The emotional vocabulary effect

Children are born with the same basic emotional hardware we all have. What varies enormously between children — and between adults — is their ability to identify, name and communicate what they're feeling. Psychologists call this emotional granularity: the capacity to distinguish between, say, frustration and disappointment, or excitement and anxiety.

The research is clear: children with larger emotional vocabularies are better at regulating their own behaviour, more empathetic towards others, and significantly less likely to express feelings through aggression or withdrawal. And one of the most reliable ways to build emotional vocabulary in young children is through stories.

When a character in a book names what they're feeling — and then navigates that feeling in a way the child can follow — the child adds that feeling to their internal map. Not as an abstract concept, but as something experienced and understood.

The problem with lessons disguised as stories

Much of children's publishing — particularly in the emotional intelligence space — makes a specific mistake. The story exists to deliver a moral. Be kind. Share your things. Tell the truth. The problem isn't the moral. The problem is that children can feel the scaffolding.

When a story is clearly built around a lesson, children engage with it differently. They're receiving information rather than experiencing something. The emotional processing that comes from genuine storytelling — the kind that changes how you see yourself and others — doesn't happen.

The books that stick are the ones that trust the child. That show rather than tell. That present a feeling honestly without rushing to resolve it. A child doesn't need Little Fox to have all the answers — they need Little Fox to have the same questions.

What to look for

When you're choosing books for young children, a few things are worth considering beyond the obvious:

  • Does the character feel the feeling fully? Not just mention it and move on, but sit with it long enough that the child recognises the experience.
  • Is the resolution realistic? Tidy endings feel safe but teach children that feelings disappear quickly if you do the right thing. They often don't.
  • Is there space for conversation? The best picture books are conversation starters, not conversation endings. Something left open is an invitation.
  • Does the child see themselves? Representation matters — not just in terms of appearance, but in terms of experience and feeling.

The long game

Reading with a child isn't primarily about literacy — though that matters enormously. It's about building, over hundreds of shared hours, a shared language for the interior life. Children who grow up with books that take feelings seriously grow into adults who take feelings seriously — their own and other people's.

That's not a small thing. The Little Fox & Friends series exists because we believe children's books can do more than entertain. They can quietly, gently, and durably change how a child understands themselves.

That's worth taking seriously from the very first page.
